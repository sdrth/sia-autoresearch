# Hypotheses

## Search strategy

- Starting question: What product changes would most reduce trial-to-paid drop-off for a B2B workflow tool?
- Default search tools used: web search plus read-only review of the repo prompt context
- Query optimization notes:
  - `trial to paid conversion product changes` was too broad, so the search was narrowed toward activation, onboarding, and first-value moments
  - `B2B SaaS onboarding drop-off patterns 2025` surfaced higher-signal evidence than generic conversion-rate articles
  - activation-specific queries outperformed pricing and feature-parity queries for explaining the drop-off

## Candidate hypotheses generated

1. Pricing friction is the main reason trials fail to convert
2. Missing activation metric is blocking all meaningful onboarding improvement
3. Empty first-login experience is the highest-leverage onboarding fix
4. Generic onboarding flow is mismatched to the user's intended job
5. Feature gaps versus competitors are the main source of churn
6. Lack of human sales touch is suppressing conversion

## Shortlisted for evidence collection

### Candidate A
- Hypothesis: Missing activation metric is the root constraint
- Why it looked promising:
  - repeated references to activation as the strongest leading indicator
  - likely to explain why teams make changes without learning
- Queries run:
  - activation metric benchmarks SaaS
  - B2B SaaS onboarding drop-off patterns 2025
- Early evidence:
  - top-performing teams define one measurable first-value event
  - activation correlates more directly with conversion than top-of-funnel volume
- Status: advanced
- Why:
  - strongest explanatory power and highest downstream leverage

### Candidate B
- Hypothesis: Empty first-login state is the highest-ROI product fix
- Why it looked promising:
  - blank-state experience shows up repeatedly in onboarding guidance
  - plausible low-effort, high-impact intervention
- Queries run:
  - SaaS blank state onboarding activation
  - trial to paid conversion product changes
- Early evidence:
  - empty products create confusion and stall first value
  - templates and guided first actions repeatedly appear as fast wins
- Status: advanced
- Why:
  - strong actionability and direct product relevance

### Candidate C
- Hypothesis: Intent-matched onboarding beats generic feature tours
- Why it looked promising:
  - user intent is often more predictive than persona labels
  - ties directly to the stated job the user signed up to do
- Queries run:
  - onboarding segmentation by user intent SaaS
  - jobs to be done onboarding B2B SaaS
- Early evidence:
  - job-specific flows reduce irrelevant setup steps
  - feature tours are often too broad for complex tools
- Status: advanced
- Why:
  - strong product angle and coherent with activation logic

### Candidate D
- Hypothesis: Pricing friction is the primary issue
- Why it looked promising:
  - common explanation for trial non-conversion
- Queries run:
  - trial pricing friction SaaS conversion
- Early evidence:
  - weak compared with activation and onboarding signals
  - little support that pricing outranks first-value problems for early-stage workflow tools
- Status: discarded
- Why:
  - evidence quality was lower and the explanation felt downstream of activation failure

### Candidate E
- Hypothesis: Feature gaps versus competitors are the main source of churn
- Why it looked promising:
  - easy founder instinct when users do not convert
- Queries run:
  - feature parity trial conversion SaaS
- Early evidence:
  - much weaker than onboarding and activation explanations
  - strong products still lose users before they ever reach value
- Status: discarded
- Why:
  - low leverage for a first response and poor fit for the observed problem framing

### Candidate F
- Hypothesis: Lack of human sales touch is suppressing conversion
- Why it looked promising:
  - relevant in some high-consideration B2B motions
- Queries run:
  - product-led growth sales assist trial conversion
- Early evidence:
  - relevant for some segments, but not a first-order explanation for onboarding drop-off
- Status: discarded
- Why:
  - lower product leverage than activation, blank state, and intent-matched onboarding

## Winning thesis

- Selected hypothesis:
  - Activation speed is the core problem. The best bets are to define one activation metric, eliminate the blank first-login state, and align onboarding to user intent.
- Why it won:
  - it explained the drop-off more completely than pricing or feature-gap narratives
  - it produced three concrete, testable product bets with strong evidence support
- What lost and why:
  - pricing friction — too downstream and weakly evidenced here
  - feature-gap theory — less explanatory than failure to reach first value
  - sales-touch theory — relevant later, but weaker than product-side onboarding fixes
