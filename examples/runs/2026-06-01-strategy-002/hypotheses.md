# Hypotheses

## Search strategy

- Starting question: Should an early-stage B2B workflow tool verticalize into one industry now, or stay horizontal until activation is fixed?
- Default search tools used: web search plus read-only review of lab memory (`notes/autoresearch.md`, run 001 findings on activation)
- Query optimization notes:
  - `vertical SaaS vs horizontal SaaS` returned mostly definitional content; adding `early stage when to verticalize` surfaced decision frameworks
  - adding `failure` and `too early` terms surfaced counter-evidence, which the first query branch lacked entirely
  - lab memory mattered: run 001 established that activation is broken, which reframed this from a market question into a sequencing question

## Candidate hypotheses generated

1. Verticalize now — a vertical wedge beats horizontal positioning at early stage
2. Stay fully horizontal until activation is fixed — verticalizing now compounds the onboarding problem
3. Verticalize marketing and templates only; keep the product horizontal
4. Verticalization is a distribution decision, not a product decision
5. Choose the vertical from existing user concentration, not market attractiveness
6. Verticalize only after a repeatable onboarding exists — sequencing gate, not strategy change

## Shortlisted for evidence collection

### Candidate A
- Hypothesis: Verticalize marketing and templates only; keep the product horizontal
- Why it looked promising:
  - avoids rebuilding the product while testing vertical demand
  - consistent with run 001's finding that blank-state templates are a top activation fix
- Queries run:
  - horizontal SaaS verticalized marketing playbook
  - vertical SaaS wedge go-to-market beachhead
- Early evidence:
  - repeated pattern: horizontal products winning vertical beachheads through templates, integrations, and messaging rather than forked codebases
  - vertical landing pages + template packs are a cheap demand test
- Status: advanced
- Why:
  - lowest-cost falsifiable test of vertical demand; composes with run 001 bets instead of competing with them

### Candidate B
- Hypothesis: Choose the vertical from existing user concentration, not market attractiveness
- Why it looked promising:
  - picking a vertical from market-size analysis alone is a common failure pattern
- Queries run:
  - SaaS beachhead selection existing customer concentration
  - picking a vertical market early stage SaaS
- Early evidence:
  - beachhead guidance consistently starts from where pull already exists
  - founders who picked verticals by TAM rather than usage signal churned through segments
- Status: advanced
- Why:
  - turns the vertical choice into a data exercise the team can run this week

### Candidate C
- Hypothesis: Verticalize now — full vertical product wedge
- Why it looked promising:
  - vertical SaaS narrative is strong in current market commentary
- Queries run:
  - vertical SaaS vs horizontal SaaS early stage when to verticalize
  - SaaS verticalization too early failure
- Early evidence:
  - vertical depth wins long-term in many categories, but the strongest cases started with working activation and a clear ICP
  - counter-evidence branch surfaced teams that verticalized to escape a weak product core and made both problems worse
- Status: discarded
- Why:
  - sequencing risk: verticalizing on top of broken activation hides the constraint instead of fixing it

### Candidate D
- Hypothesis: Stay fully horizontal until activation is fixed; revisit in two quarters
- Why it looked promising:
  - cleanest reading of run 001 — fix the funnel before changing the market
- Queries run:
  - focus vs expansion sequencing early stage SaaS
- Early evidence:
  - real, but ignores that vertical templates and messaging are themselves activation levers
- Status: discarded
- Why:
  - treats vertical and activation work as competing when the evidence says they compose — the pure-wait position was weaker than the hybrid

### Candidate E
- Hypothesis: Verticalization is a distribution decision, not a product decision
- Why it looked promising:
  - reframe that explains why template/messaging verticalization works without forking the product
- Queries run:
  - vertical SaaS wedge go-to-market beachhead
- Early evidence:
  - supported as a framing, but not independently actionable
- Status: discarded (merged)
- Why:
  - absorbed into the winning thesis as its core framing rather than standing alone

## Winning thesis

- Selected hypothesis:
  - Verticalize the wedge, not the product: keep the horizontal core, pick one beachhead vertical from existing user concentration, and ship vertical templates + messaging — sequenced after the activation metric from run 001 is instrumented.
- Why it won:
  - it composes with run 001 instead of competing with it (templates double as the blank-state fix)
  - it converts a strategy debate into two cheap, falsifiable tests (concentration analysis, vertical landing page + template pack)
  - it survived the counter-evidence branch that killed the "verticalize now" position
- What lost and why:
  - full verticalization now — sequencing risk on top of broken activation; strongest counter-evidence
  - pure wait — ignores that vertical assets are themselves activation levers
  - distribution-reframe alone — a framing, not a plan; merged into the winner
